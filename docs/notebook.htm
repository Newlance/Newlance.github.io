The Country Notebook – Russia

By Evan Lawrence

 

Executive Report

As you can see in Figure 1, Russia understands and accepts the importance of having superiors much more than America.  Judging by the graph, it is clear America has a much higher sense of self-dependence, whereas Russia is a collective society who sees the need to depend on one another.  America scores nearly twice as high on the Masculinity dimension, indicating we are much more goal oriented, with less focus on happiness and quality of life.  Russia more than doubles America on the dimension of Uncertainty avoidance, indicating Americans are much more comfortable facing the unknown than Russians.  America’s score of 29 on the Long term orientation dimension places itself over 10 times higher than that of Russia, who scores a nearly invisible 2.88.  This indicates Americas much higher desire to evolve and look to the future, while Russia prefers remaining true to their heritage.

            Russia is the largest country in the world.  With nearly 14% of Russia’s landmass being Agricultural land farms have had an excellent opportunity to grow and flourish.  Russia’s intellectual capital is also one of the world’s greatest, producing the highest amount of college graduates worldwide.  Despite these facts businesses interested in growing in Russia are faced with little competition.  The potential for growth is great; however, due to the word’s general opinion of the country, many companies have not attempted to move here.  Until the early 90’s, Russia was a communist nation, only then did Russia begin converting to capitalism.  While this infantile state poses an obstacle for potential businesses, it should be known that most Russian business are eager to embrace Westernized business practices.  The challenge with this, however, is that Russia has a strong attachment to historical conventions.  This coupled with their high levels of uncertainty avoidance can cause difficulties in business relations.  While they are eager for the rewards of capitalism, many of our business practices intimidate or offend, causing the loss of potential business deals.

Russia is among the top 10% countries for power distance, with a score of 93.  This is likely a lasting effect from Russia’s years of being a communist nation.  Russian workers are comfortable knowing their leaders are superior, and will rarely question them.  On the other hand, when taking charge, a business leader must quickly and effectively demonstrate his or her competence. There is a vast amount of diversity in Russia, in both language and religion.  The primary official language of Russia is Russian; however, there are 27 other official languages, and over 100 minority languages spoken.  Figure 3 depicts the religions practiced within Russia and the percentages of people who practice them.  There is a wide variety of religions in Russia; however, Russian Orthodox seems to be the most widely practiced religion in Russia.

            For a long time Russia has produced some of the finest athletes in the world.  Some of the most noteworthy competitions took place in weightlifting, boxing, ice hockey, gymnastics, chess,  and wrestling.

The Russian unemployment rate is 5.6%, lower than the US who’s unemployment rate is 7.5%.  With 71,590,000 and 10% of that in the agricultural business, Russia has a fairly stable economy.  The work week is not much different than that of the US.  The work week consists of 40 hours spread Monday-Saturday, 8 hours a day.  Sunday is always a holiday (with exceptions for some essential businesses/services) and the employer chooses one other holiday day (usually Saturday).

Russia is a federal semi-presidential republic. Federal means it has a strong, centralized federal government that can mint money, provide for national defense, set tariffs, etc. (much like the US). Semi-presidential means it has both a president and a prime minister, and the cabinet answers to the parliament (in US, the cabinet only answers to the president). Republic means it is a representative government, in which people vote for a representative rather than directly voting on laws/issues (just like in the US). 

Russia trades on the international market.  With the US alone, Russia imports nearly 3 times as much as it exports.  On a global scale, energy/mineral products account for more than 60% of their exports; while their largest import, machinery and equipment, accounts for just over 40% of their imports.

Despite Russia’s appearance of poverty, most families are home owners, and only 10% of all apartments are without heat/hot water.  Over 30% of the population has access to private internet.  Russia has over 2,000 airports, as well as many seaports, and over 87,000km of railroad (second largest railroad system in the world).  More than 80% of Russia’s 933,000km of roads are paved, as well.

As with any foreign country, business affairs must be approached with the country’s customs in mind.  Russia is very particular about certain things, and an accidental gesture of disrespect could be enough to end a potential negotiation, even if they believe the deal to be in their best interest.  It is always important to be firm, yet approachable, friendly, but not overly familiar.  It is customary to get to know a potential business partner on a personal level before doing business.  It is appropriate to bring gifts, and may be in your best interest to get to know each other over a drink.  Russians are attempting to adopt capitalism and therefore will expect more of a Westerner.  However modern they are attempting to become, it is still advisable to send a male business negotiator as females are still considered the weaker sex in Russia.

Russia's long-term challenges include a shrinking workforce, a high level of corruption, difficulty in accessing capital for smaller, non-energy companies and poor infrastructure in need of large investments.
